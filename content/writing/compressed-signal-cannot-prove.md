Give two agents a shared space to explore and let each cover a handful of regions in it. Neither one reports back the regions themselves - too much data for a channel this narrow - only a compressed signal derived from them. A dispatcher reading just the signals is trying to answer one thing - whether these two agents covered ground worth combining, or mostly retread each other. Here is a case where the signal cannot answer that, worked out by hand before any of the math does the same work.

| Pair | Agent 1 explored | Agent 2 explored | Shared ground | Regions that differ | Genuinely complementary |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | 1, 2 | 3, 4 | none | 1, 2, 3, 4 | Yes - no overlap at all |
| B | 1, 2, 3 | 1, 4, 5 | 1 | 2, 3, 4, 5 | No - overlaps at region 1 |

The signal each agent sends back is built to preserve one number - how far apart two exploration records sit, the count of regions where they disagree. Pair A's two agents disagree at all four regions between them, because neither one went anywhere the other went. Pair B's agents also disagree at four regions, but one of the regions where they agree is real shared ground, region 1, work both of them had already done, and that changes what the four means. A dispatcher holding only the number 4 cannot tell these two pairs apart. Pair A is exactly the case worth combining, two explorations sharing nothing, so merging them adds ground neither one had alone. Pair B retreads itself before the difference even starts, and reading the number 4 more carefully never recovers that fact, because the number was never carrying it in the first place.

---

This gap surfaces in a real design whenever many independent processes report back over a channel too narrow to carry everything they saw. The channel is narrow on purpose. Compressing a working state by a factor in the tens of thousands to one isn't an oversight here - it's the reason a compressed signal gets built instead of shipping the full state between agents. What the shrunk signal has to carry is whatever a receiver needs for the one decision worth making with it, and that decision is not how similar two agents' work looks. It's whether combining what they found produces something neither one found alone - the complementarity question the table above just answered by hand. A real protocol has to answer it from the signal alone, at scale, with nobody reading the underlying regions.

This isn't hypothetical. It's a live claim in the formal registry for Gnomon, a private architecture project of mine, the same registry behind [The falsification tests that killed Gnomon's central bet](/writing/gnomon-falsification-tests/). The architecture exists as a specification - every load-bearing claim in it tagged proved, empirical, or conjectural, not as running code. Claim 3.5 in that registry concerns a different mechanism than the one those tests killed - not whether a teacher model's internal routing can be extracted, but whether the compressed signal that many small worker agents exchange while exploring can support a decision about whether their work is worth combining. It splits into three parts, at three different levels of certainty.

---

The first, Claim 3.5a, tagged proved, shows the signal has room to spare for half the job. The result behind it, the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma, says a set of N points can be projected into roughly 8·ε⁻²·ln(N) dimensions and keep every pairwise distance within a factor of (1±ε) of the original - compression that costs distortion, not information, once the target space has enough room for the number of points going into it. Applied at the largest single group the design needs to hold at once, 250 records, held to 10 percent distortion, that bound asks for at least 4,416 dimensions. The signal the specification allots is 64 bytes, 512 bits total, nowhere near that number, and the proof doesn't need it to be - the full theorem isn't doing the work here. What the signal is projecting isn't an agent's whole working state. It's a position on a small shared map - which of 56 possible processing configurations the agent used, about six bits, plus two scalars for how converged and how novel that position is. Eight dimensions, generously counted, and 512 bits is far more room than eight dimensions will ever need at any precision worth having.

The one place in the design where the full theorem earns its keep rather than sitting there as overkill is a coarser signal one level up - twelve cluster summaries, compressed to a 256-byte budget. The same bound applied there asks for roughly 1,986 dimensions at one bit apiece, about 249 bytes. Two hundred fifty-six is what the design allows. Tight, and proved sufficient regardless of how little slack it leaves.

---

The second, Claim 3.5b, tagged proved negative, shows that sufficiency doesn't come free, by a route with nothing to do with this particular design. Kalyanasundaram and Schnitger, and independently Razborov, established a lower bound on the two-party communication complexity of Set Disjointness. Deciding whether two parties' sets share any element at all requires each side to send, on average, a number of bits proportional to n, the size of the universe the sets are drawn from - no cleverness gets around it, because the back-and-forth genuinely needed to settle disjointness provably cannot compress below that floor.

A distance-preserving signal is not that back-and-forth. It is a fixed, one-shot projection, and the reason it can't rescue disjointness is exactly the mechanism the table at the top of this piece put in front of you by hand. The squared distance a Johnson-Lindenstrauss embedding preserves reduces, for signals built from region membership, to Hamming distance - the count of positions where two records disagree - and Hamming distance counts total disagreement without asking whether any of that disagreement sits on ground the two records also share. Pair A and Pair B measured the same distance and answered the disjointness question in opposite directions. That wasn't a pair contrived to embarrass the bound. It's the kind of pair the bound says has to exist, for any distance-preserving scheme, somewhere in its input space, because a proof that a decision needs n bits and a mechanism that provably reduces every input to a handful of bits derived from far fewer than n cannot both be describing the same computation.

---

Both proofs are about the same 64 bytes, and neither one is wrong. The first proves the signal is sufficient for a genuine, specified property - recovering how far apart two positions are, within a stated tolerance, at a stated confidence. The second proves the signal is insufficient for a different property a design that calls this channel "awareness" might assume comes bundled with the first one for free - whether two agents' work is worth combining. Bandwidth sufficient for one guarantee is not decision sufficiency for another, and no amount of compressing the signal better closes that gap, because the gap was never about compression quality to begin with.

[The checkpoint that hashed correctly and still lost a file](/writing/binding-not-preservation/) runs into the identical shape from a different direction entirely, a release ledger where a digest match proved one thing about a checkpoint and got trusted, silently, for a second thing it had never checked. The lesson generalizes past both cases. A proof of sufficiency names exactly the property it covers and covers nothing else, no matter how load-bearing the property it leaves out turns out to be.

---

An earlier design pass on this same architecture ran into the gap before either proof existed to name it. [Coordination that isn't voting](/writing/coordination-that-isnt-voting/) describes a lateral-awareness mechanism that a design review found had quietly centralized itself, collect-then-aggregate wearing the name "shared awareness," diagnosed there as solving a bandwidth problem by deleting the mechanism it was supposed to carry. The diagnosis was correct, and at the time it was informal. Here is the formal version.

Building that mechanism under a real byte budget means reaching for exactly the tool the first proof says works - a small, distance-preserving signal, provably enough bits for what it promises. Nothing about that choice is visibly wrong. The signal does preserve distance, faithfully, at the stated tolerance. What it cannot do is answer the complementarity question the mechanism exists to answer, because the second proof holds regardless of how carefully the signal gets built - no fixed-size, one-shot projection settles disjointness at the certainty a real decision needs, over the inputs where the question is genuinely hard. A mechanism that answers the wrong question well still gets used, until the mismatch surfaces, and the fix the design settled on routed the decision back to a point holding the full, uncompressed state. That's centralization by construction, whatever the mechanism is still named. The name survived the redesign. The lateral property it was supposed to carry did not.

---

The registry doesn't stop at the negative result, and it doesn't treat the negative result as the end of the story either. The third part, Claim 3.5c, is marked conjectural, and stays that way in the text. If complementarity varies smoothly with position on the shared map - if two agents whose signals sit close together are reliably about as complementary as two whose signals sit far apart, and that relationship doesn't jump around - then approximate complementarity is recoverable from a signal this small after all, at a cost closer to log N bits than the n bits the exact version needs.

The argument for that assumption is architectural - which configuration an agent used determines the kind of processing it did, so two agents using disjoint configurations are exploring structurally different territory, and distance on the map should track difference in kind. The argument against it is a specific failure mode the registry names directly - two agents whose configurations differ by exactly one choice can turn out to be the most complementary pair in the whole group, if that one choice happens to be the one carrying the distinction that mattered, in which case nearby signals hide the largest disagreement instead of predicting agreement. Nothing in the specification decides which of those two arguments wins. The falsifier is pre-registered - rank correlation between signal distance and measured complementarity, checked against a floor of 0.3, at a milestone named in the build plan before the benchmark that would produce the number exists. That benchmark hasn't run. The registry lists this claim as conjectural because that's what it currently is.
